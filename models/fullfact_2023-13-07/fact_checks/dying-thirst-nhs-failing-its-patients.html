There are many reasons why someone's kidneys might stop functioning and they might develop what's known asÂ acute kidney injuryÂ (or AKI). Dehydration is one possible cause.

Acute kidney injury - avoidable deaths

If your kidneys malfunction, there's no way for your body to clean your blood. AKI develops easily when people are already ill and their immune system is weakened.Â 

The Daily Mail's statistic of "42,000 avoidable deaths" is one thatÂ the National Institute of Health and Care Excellence (NICE) has put its name to, as it informed us today. However, NICE's ownÂ press release actually mentions a more conservative figure: 12,000.

What's actually being claimed is thatÂ betweenÂ 12,000 and 42,000 deaths are avoidable each year.

This calculation is based on a factsheet from the Kidney Alliance, which cites estimates that of 5.2 million emergency admissions in the UK (in 2011/12), between 5% and 20% are likely to be AKI (since it isn't always detected, we end up with a wide range).Â So that's between 262,000 and one million cases of AKI each year.Â 

On top of that, it's estimated thatÂ 24% of patients with AKI will die from the condition. That results in between 62,000 and 210,000 deathsÂ from AKI each year.

Finally, drawing on a 2009 study, about a fifth of all deaths from AKI were considered avoidable. This leaves us with between 12,000 and 42,000 avoidable deaths each year.

While the Daily Mail has featured the higher figure suggested by NICE,Â other sections of the mediaÂ have preferred to quote the lower estimate.Â 

Numbers dying of thirst in hundreds, not thousands

While NICE has calculated that up to 42,000 people might be dying of kidney-related illnesses, we can't say that all of these people have died of thirst.Â 

The Office for National Statistics (ONS), which produces data on different causes of death, calculatesÂ the number of deaths from dehydrationÂ (where this is specifically mentioned on the death certificate).

What NICE (and the Daily Mail) suggest is that thousands of patients are dying from AKI without anyone realising. It's not that patients are necessarily developing the condition in hospital, but doctors and nurses are in many cases failing to detect it and treat it appropriately. A simple blood test will confirm whether or not someone is suffering from AKI.

The Daily Mail is not a lone voice when it suggests that - whatever angle you look at it from - there's a problem with how patients are being cared for. A 2009 study into deaths from AKI concluded that only a third of those who'd developed the condition in hospital received "good care".Â While a lack of water might contribute to a patient's deterioration, they will also need to be given medication and any potential infection will need to be treated.

While the Daily Mail has emphasised the risk of AKI patients dying from dehydration, there's no direct evidence to prove that thousands of deaths are due to this specific cause.Â
